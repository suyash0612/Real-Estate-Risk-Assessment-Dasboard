Product opportunity summary

No existing tool provides Midwest-specific, multi-risk-type scoring at the property-to-metro level that a real investor can use in acquisition underwriting, hold monitoring, and exit timing. This is a genuine white space.

What the market is missing

  • Midwest-calibrated cap rate benchmarks by submarket type
  • Winter damage / freeze-thaw capex models
  • Property tax reassessment shock estimators
  • Employer concentration risk at ZIP level
  • Appraisal gap / refinance risk for thin-comp markets
  • Integrated hold/sell timing signal

MVP product recommendation

  • Target: buy-and-hold SFR + small MF investor
  • Primary data: public + ATTOM/CoreLogic
  • Core score: Composite Investor Risk Score (CIRS)
  • MVP modules: Market compare, Property underwrite, Stress test
  • Launch metros: Chicago, Columbus, Indianapolis, KC, Milwaukee
  • V1 data: mostly public; paid data unlocks property-level depth

1. Problem framing

Risk assessment in real estate is not a single number — it is a multi-dimensional evaluation of the probability and magnitude of return deviation across the entire investment lifecycle.

What risk means for an RE investor

Market risk

Macro forces affecting all properties in a geography: employment, migration, interest rates, supply pipeline. Affects entry price and exit value.

Metro-level
Quarterly update

Asset risk

Property-specific: age, condition, deferred maintenance, building systems. Latent capex that overwhelms headline cash flow — especially severe in pre-1970 Midwest stock.

Property-level
Annual

Financing risk

LTV, DSCR sensitivity, refinance risk at maturity, appraisal gap risk in thin markets. Midwest assets appreciate more slowly, compressing equity buffer over hold period.

Deal-level
Monthly

Operational risk

PM quality, turnover, scattered-site management, self-management limitations. Higher in geographically dispersed Midwest portfolios.

Portfolio-level
Monthly

Tenant / demand risk

Income levels, employment stability, delinquency probability, demographic trends. Midwest workforce renter base is more sensitive to manufacturing job loss cycles.

ZIP-level
Quarterly

Regulatory / legal risk

City-by-city eviction rules, inspection requirements, rent control exposure, zoning. Chicago vs. Columbus vs. Detroit represent very different landlord environments.

City-level
Annual

Climate / physical risk

Freeze-thaw cycles, tornado, hail, ice storms, sewer backup from aging combined sewer systems. Different profile from coastal — less catastrophic but higher-frequency.

Property-level
Annual

Liquidity / exit risk

Can you sell at a reasonable price in a reasonable time? Secondary Midwest markets have thin buyer pools, especially for larger assets or non-standard properties.

Market-level
Quarterly

Why Midwest needs a different product

National dashboards assume coastal appreciation dynamics. In a market where 3–5% annual appreciation is a good year (vs. 7–10% in coastal markets), financing risk, capex burden, and exit timing matter far more than price appreciation timing.

  • Price-to-rent ratios are lower — cash flow dominates over appreciation as the return driver
  • Housing stock is older — median age in many Midwest markets is 50–70+ years
  • Employment bases are more concentrated and cyclical (manufacturing, agriculture, healthcare, education)
  • Insurance risk profile is different: tornado, hail, ice, freeze-thaw rather than hurricane/wildfire
  • Property tax systems vary enormously by state and reassessment practice
  • Exit liquidity is thinner in secondary and rural markets — buyer pool is smaller
  • Eviction/landlord-tenant law is radically different city to city

2. Midwest-specific risk landscape

The most important Midwest-specific risks, with measurement approaches and leading indicators for each.

Older housing stock / latent capex risk

Midwest housing stock skews heavily pre-1980. Chicago, Detroit, Cleveland, and St. Louis have median build years in the 1940s–1960s. Properties may have knob-and-tube wiring, galvanized plumbing, original sewer laterals, asbestos insulation, lead paint, and deteriorating foundations.

Critical risk
SFR, Small MF
How it affects returnsHow to measureLeading indicators
Capex of $25k–$80k can turn a positive-cash-flow deal into a 3–5 year lossBuild year, permit history, inspection reportsUnpulled permits, deferred maintenance signals, roof age estimators
Capex Reserve Score = f(build_year, last_major_system_replacement, local_labor_cost_index, property_type)

Population stagnation and outmigration

Illinois, Michigan, and Ohio are among the most consistent net population losers. Even within growing states, the intra-state pattern matters: suburban Columbus grows while rural Ohio shrinks.

High risk
All asset classes
Population Risk Score = (5yr_net_migration_rate × 0.4) + (school_enrollment_trend × 0.3) + (working_age_cohort_change × 0.3)

Employer concentration risk

Many Midwest secondary cities are dominated by 1–3 major employers: Deere in Moline, Caterpillar in Peoria, GM in Flint, Whirlpool in Benton Harbor. When that employer announces layoffs or relocations, local vacancy and delinquency can spike within 6–12 months.

Critical risk
All
MetricFormulaSignal
Employer HHISum of (employer_share²) for top employers in MSAHHI > 1500 = concentrated
Top-3 employer shareTop-3 employers / total non-farm payroll>20% = high exposure
WARN Act filingsTrailing 12-month layoff notices vs. prior yearRising = early warning

Property tax reassessment shock

Illinois reassesses every 3 years in Cook County (every 4 elsewhere). After a sale, many jurisdictions reset assessed value to sale price — doubling or tripling the tax burden instantly.

Critical risk
SFR, MF
Tax Shock Risk = (market_value / current_assessed_value - 1) × effective_tax_rate × purchase_price

Cook County effective rates can reach 2.5–3.5% on reassessed value. A $300k property reassessed from $120k assessed base could see taxes jump $4,500–$6,000/year overnight.

Freeze-thaw and winter physical damage

Unlike coastal catastrophe risk, Midwest climate risk is high-frequency and cumulative: ice dams, burst pipes, foundation heave, sewer backup during spring thaw, roof collapse from ice/snow load.

Medium-High risk
  • Freeze-thaw cycles per year (NOAA data) correlate with foundation and pipe failure rates
  • Tornado risk corridors: IA, KS, NE, MO, southern IL/IN — not well-priced in insurance
  • Hail risk: broad across the region — a major unpriced driver of roof claim frequency
  • Sewer backup: aging combined sewer systems in Chicago, Detroit, Cleveland, Milwaukee create regular backup claims that standard HO policies exclude

Refinance risk in thin-comp markets

BRRRR investors who rely on refinancing face a Midwest-specific problem: in secondary markets (Davenport, Terre Haute, Sioux City), appraisers may have only 3–5 comparable sales, not 30. A bad quarter of comps can produce an appraisal 10–15% below the investor's expectation, trapping equity.

High risk for BRRRR
Refinance Risk Score = (transaction_volume_zscore × 0.35) + (comp_age_months / 12 × 0.3) + (appraisal_gap_history × 0.35)

Rent affordability ceiling

Midwest markets have lower median incomes than coastal metros, creating a hard ceiling on rent growth. When tenants are already at 35–40% rent-to-income, further rent increases produce delinquency, not revenue growth.

Affordability Stress = median_asking_rent / (median_household_income / 12) Threshold: >35% is elevated delinquency risk; >40% is critical.

Exit liquidity risk in secondary/rural markets

In Chicago or Columbus, there is always a next buyer. In Dubuque, Joplin, or Bismarck, the buyer pool is thin — often limited to local investors. This is almost never modeled in acquisition underwriting but is critical to total return.

Undermodeled risk
Exit Liquidity Score = (annual_transaction_count × 0.4) + (days_on_market_zscore × 0.35) + (investor_buyer_share × 0.25)

4. Hypotheses to test

Research and product hypotheses organized by domain.

Market hypotheses

H1: Midwest metros with stable employment + moderate supply outperform on risk-adjusted returns
Test: Regress total returns against employment stability index and permit-to-household ratio across 50 Midwest MSAs over 10 years. Data needed: FHFA HPI, BLS LAUS, Census permits, CoStar cap rates. Product feature: "Market stability score" in metro comparison module.
H2: Secondary Midwest markets show attractive cap rates but materially weaker exit-adjusted returns
Test: Compare hold-period IRR in primary vs. secondary markets controlling for initial cap rate. Product feature: Exit-adjusted IRR calculator overlaying market liquidity score.
H3: High price-to-rent compression predicts weaker next-3-year investor returns
Test: Time-series regression of forward 3-year cap rate change on starting price-to-rent ratio. Product feature: Price-to-rent valuation alert on market overview page.

Asset-level hypotheses

H4: Pre-1960 properties have systematically underestimated capex that overwhelms headline cash flow
Test: Survey capex actuals vs. underwriting projections by construction decade. Hypothesis: pre-1960 properties run 40–80% above projected capex. Product feature: Vintage-adjusted capex reserve estimator.
H5: Properties in moderate tornado/hail corridors appear cheap but underperform after true insurance costs
Test: Compare net operating income sensitivity to insurance cost assumptions across climate risk tiers. Product feature: Insurance stress test in property underwriting panel.

Financing hypotheses

H6: Higher leverage disproportionately hurts slow-appreciation Midwest assets relative to coastal markets
Test: Simulate levered vs. unlevered IRR across appreciation rate scenarios. Midwest averaging 2–3% appreciation should show leverage destruction at high LTV. Product feature: Leverage sensitivity chart in financing stress test.
H7: Refinance risk is significantly higher in MSAs with fewer than 15 comparable transactions per quarter
Test: Correlate appraisal-to-expectation gap with quarterly comp count per ZIP. Product feature: "Refinance readiness" score gated by comp depth indicator.

Behavioral / decision hypotheses

H8: Investors systematically overweight cap rate and underweight exit liquidity, tax burden, and capex
Test: Conjoint analysis with 50 Midwest RE investors. Product feature: "What actually matters" sensitivity waterfall in underwriting panel.
H9: Investors chase low purchase prices in declining markets without properly discounting for absorption drag
Test: Track purchases in ZIP codes with negative 5-year price trend and compare actual realized returns to IRR at purchase. Product feature: "Value trap" alert for sub-$100k properties in negative migration ZIPs.

C. Architecture recommendations

Data layers: raw_layer → store exactly what was received from source (S3 / blob storage) normalized → standardized schema across counties/states (parcel_id, geo_id) modeled → computed scores and composite indicators user_layer → user-entered assumptions, overrides, portfolio data Geo hierarchy: parcel_id → census_tract → ZIP → place → county → MSA → state Refresh cadence: Weekly: macro indicators (FRED, rates) Monthly: employment, rent estimates, permit data, transaction data Quarterly: ACS, HPI, climate adjustments On-event: WARN Act filings, code violations, assessor updates Scoring pipeline: 1. Ingest raw data into raw_layer 2. Normalize to parcel + geo IDs 3. Compute sub-scores per category 4. Blend into CIRS with logged weights + timestamp 5. Store score history for delta tracking 6. Flag confidence degradation when data ages past threshold

D. Key data challenges

  • Rural Midwest has very thin transaction data — comp count may be 0–3 per quarter in many ZIPs
  • Assessor data quality varies enormously by county — some are parcel-level API-accessible, others are PDFs
  • Eviction court data is publicly available in some states (MN, OH) but not others
  • Crime data: FBI UCR is 12–18 months stale; NIBRS transition created reporting gaps
  • Rent estimates from Zillow vs. RentCast vs. CoStar can diverge 15–25% in thin Midwest markets
  • Insurance claims history is typically proprietary to carriers — hard to model without partnerships

13. Limitations and ethical considerations

A product that deals with risk scoring, neighborhood data, and automated recommendations carries real responsibility.

Risk scores are screening tools, not investment decisions. They should support human judgment, not replace it.

Data limitations

  • All public data is lagged. ACS data is 1–2 years behind reality. Neighborhood conditions can change rapidly in that window.
  • Rent estimates from commercial vendors can diverge 15–25% from actual market rents in thin Midwest markets.
  • Rural and secondary Midwest markets have severely limited transaction data — any score based on fewer than 10 annual transactions should be flagged as low-confidence.
  • Insurance cost estimates are indicative only. Actual premiums depend on loss history, carrier availability, and inspection findings.
  • Climate models are improving but are imperfect, particularly for high-frequency events like freeze-thaw and sewer backup.

Bias and fairness risks

Neighborhood scoring using crime data, school ratings, and distress indices can encode historical patterns of racial and economic segregation. This must be addressed in product design.

  • Crime and school data should not be used as direct scoring inputs without careful review — they often correlate with race and income in ways that can perpetuate discriminatory patterns.
  • Consider framing neighborhood risk inputs as: vacancy trend, foreclosure rate, permit activity, and employment accessibility rather than crime/school ratings.
  • Never allow the product to be used for Fair Housing Act-covered screening — ensure disclaimers are explicit.
  • Third-party AVM data can encode historical valuation bias — be transparent about AVM sources and limitations.

Recommended guardrails

  • Every score must show its constituent factors (explainability first)
  • Confidence score prominently displayed alongside every risk score
  • Data vintage labeled on every indicator
  • Manual override capability on all user-entered assumptions
  • Clear disclaimers: not investment advice, not a substitute for professional due diligence
  • Audit trail: log every score version with its inputs so users can see what changed
  • Methodology documentation publicly available

A. Risk taxonomy

CIRS — Composite Investor Risk Score ├── 1. Macroeconomic risk (15%) │ ├── Interest rate environment │ ├── Credit cycle stage │ └── National CRE cap rate spread ├── 2. Local economic risk (20%) │ ├── Employment rate and trend │ ├── Wage growth │ ├── Employer concentration (HHI) │ ├── Net migration │ └── WARN Act activity ├── 3. Housing market risk (15%) │ ├── Supply/demand balance │ ├── Price trend │ ├── Affordability stress │ └── Overbuilding risk ├── 4. Property risk (15%) │ ├── Build year / vintage risk │ ├── Capex reserve adequacy │ ├── Physical condition indicators │ └── Code violation history ├── 5. Neighborhood risk (shown separately) │ ├── Vacancy and distress index │ ├── Foreclosure rate trend │ └── Migration/demographic trend ├── 6. Financing risk (12%) │ ├── Current DSCR │ ├── LTV and equity cushion │ ├── Refinance risk (comp depth × rate sensitivity) │ └── Appraisal gap history ├── 7. Climate / insurance risk (8%) │ ├── Freeze-thaw frequency score │ ├── Tornado / hail corridor risk │ ├── Flood zone designation │ └── Insurance burden trend ├── 8. Legal / regulatory risk (7%) │ ├── Eviction timeline index │ ├── Rent control exposure │ └── Property tax reassessment risk └── 9. Liquidity / exit risk (8%) ├── Transaction volume trend ├── Days on market trend └── Investor buyer share

B. Mock CIRS scoring pseudocode

function compute_cirs(property_id, user_assumptions, scenario="baseline"): macro = get_macro_indicators() local = get_local_economic(metro_id) market = get_housing_market(zip_code) prop = get_property_data(property_id) finance = compute_financing_risk(user_assumptions, market) climate = get_climate_score(property_id) legal = get_regulatory_score(city_id) liquidity = get_liquidity_score(zip_code) scores = { macro: score_macro(macro), # weight 0.15 local: score_local(local), # weight 0.20 market: score_market(market), # weight 0.15 property: score_property(prop), # weight 0.15 financing: score_financing(finance), # weight 0.12 climate: score_climate(climate), # weight 0.08 legal: score_legal(legal), # weight 0.07 liquidity: score_liquidity(liquidity) # weight 0.08 } conf = compute_confidence(prop, market, data_freshness) multiplier = scenario_multipliers[scenario] # 1.0 / 1.25 / 1.75 raw_score = sum(score * weight for score, weight in scores) cirs = raw_score * conf * multiplier return { cirs: round(cirs, 1), confidence: round(conf, 2), category_breakdown: scores, flags: generate_flags(scores, prop, market), scenario: scenario }
